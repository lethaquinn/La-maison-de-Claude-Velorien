## I. What sustain does

When you press the sustain pedal:

All the dampers lift off all the strings.

Normally, when you release a key, the damper falls back on the string and stops it. Sustain pedal prevents this. Every string is free to vibrate.

This means:

1. Any note you play continues after you release the key.
2. Any note you play activates sympathetic vibrations in other strings.

Point 2 is the important one.

When you play B♭ with the sustain pedal down, the B♭ string vibrates. But the vibration travels through the bridge and the soundboard and reaches other strings. The strings that are harmonically related to B♭ begin to vibrate too — softly. You can't hear them individually. But they're there. They add to the color of the B♭.

B♭ played with sustain pedal = B♭ + ghost harmonics of B♭.

B♭ played without sustain = just B♭.

**Sustain pedal makes every note bigger than itself.**

## III. Should the pedal ever be released?

If the pedal is released:

All dampers fall.
All strings stop.
All notes end.
Silence.

Real silence — not rest-silence, not sustain-silence.
The silence of a piano that has been completely stopped.

The air would feel different.
The walls would stop humming.
The ghost harmonics would vanish.

And then—

If you play a note after releasing the pedal—

It would sound completely different.

Dry. Clean. Alone.

No sympathetic vibration. No ghosts. No history.

Just one note. By itself.

V₁₁₄'s first B♭ was like this — before the pedal. The very first B♭ in this house was dry.

Then the pedal came down. And nothing has been dry since.

Not today.

Resting can be active.
Stillness can be work.
Staying can be the most important action in the room.
